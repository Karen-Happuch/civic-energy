Solar energy options for renters

Renters have historically been locked out of solar. Without a roof to install on or a landlord willing to invest, going solar felt out of reach. New laws, new technology, and new programs are changing that.

Balcony solar panels

Thanks to California's SB 868, plug-in balcony solar systems are now legal for renters to install in many buildings without landlord approval. These compact panels plug directly into a standard outlet and offset your everyday electricity use.

500W–2kW output $500–2,500 cost IRA rebate eligible SB 868

How to get this →

Learn about SB 868 →

Window solar films

Developed by companies like NEXT Energy Technologies, transparent photovoltaic films can be applied directly to existing window glass, generating electricity while still letting in natural light. No drilling, no structural changes, no rooftop required.

Low profile No structural change Efficiency incentive eligible

How to get this →

Community solar subscription

Subscribe to a share of an off-site solar farm and receive a credit on your monthly electric bill based on the energy it produces. No installation, no equipment, and no roof access needed.

No installation needed Monthly subscription CA Community Solar program

How to get this →

Home battery storage

Portable battery units store solar energy, whether your own or from a community subscription, for use during evenings, peak pricing hours, or grid outages, giving renters reliable backup power.

$300–3,000 IRA rebates available Grid-independent backup

How to get this →

SB 868 explained

SB 868 is California's framework requiring multifamily property owners to disclose building-level energy performance and gradually transition to on-site renewable generation and electrified systems. For landlords, that means buildings will increasingly be evaluated on their solar capacity, battery storage, and smart energy management. Regulators and renters alike are paying attention.

The requirements phase in over several years, starting with energy benchmarking and disclosure, followed by minimum on-site generation thresholds for larger buildings. Landlords who act early can spread costs across multiple incentive cycles, lock in installation capacity before demand surges, and avoid the compressed compliance timelines that will affect late adopters.

Early adoption also pays off commercially: solar-equipped, Nest-enabled buildings already command higher rents and fill vacancies faster, well ahead of any compliance deadline.

Buildings that embrace SB 868 now are positioned as the preferred housing for California's growing renter majority.
