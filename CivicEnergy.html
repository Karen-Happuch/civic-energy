How It Works

Two ways to bring Civic Energy into apartment living.

Portable Solar

Movable solar for renters and residents who want savings, backup power, and simple everyday use.

Step 1

See your energy use

Civic Energy Monitor reads real-time electricity use and sends it to the Google Nest energy dashboard.

Step 2

Charge with sunlight

Place your portable solar where it can receive sunlight and store energy for later use.

Step 3

Plug in directly

Connect your electrical appliances, devices, or gadgets directly to use stored solar power.

Step 4

Track what you save

View battery level, energy produced, estimated savings, and reduced grid use.

Portable solar is best for movable use, backup power, and selected energy needs. Plug-in balcony solar depends on local rules and safety standards.

Examples of Portable Solar

Different ways renters and residents can access solar without a full building installation.

Senate Bill 868 (SB 868) Plug-In Solar

California policy pathway for portable solar generation devices.

Balcony Solar Kit

Panels designed for balcony or patio use where local rules allow.

Portable Solar Panel

Movable solar panel that can be placed near sunlight and used with a power unit.

Solar Power Station

Portable battery system that stores solar energy for later use.

Window-Mounted Solar

Compact solar option designed for apartment windows or sunny indoor edges.

Foldable Solar Charger

Lightweight solar option for flexible charging and backup use.

Examples are for prototype testing. Availability, safety rules, and installation requirements may vary.

Installation Solar

Professionally installed solar for apartment owners, facility managers, and building-wide energy savings.

Step 1

Install building solar

Solar is installed on the roof, windows, facade, or approved building surface.

Step 2

Connect to the building

The system connects to an inverter, electrical panel, meter, or approved energy infrastructure.

Step 3

Use solar first

The building uses available solar power, while Pacific Gas and Electric (PG&E) or grid electricity fills the gap automatically.

Step 4

Track usage and savings

Civic Energy Monitor sends real-time electricity use to the Google Nest energy dashboard.

Step 5

Share the benefits

Owners see building value and energy performance. Renters see savings, usage insights, and cleaner apartment living.

Users do not manually switch between solar and Pacific Gas and Electric (PG&E). The system uses solar when available and pulls from the grid when needed.

Examples of Installation Solar

Building-level systems that require professional setup.

Rooftop Solar

Solar panels installed on the building roof.

Window Solar

Solar glass or window-based systems that generate energy from building windows.

Facade Solar

Solar integrated into exterior building surfaces.

Building Battery Storage

Stores solar or grid energy for later use, peak hours, or outages.

Meter-Connected Solar

Solar connected to the building meter and electrical system.

Shared Building Solar

Solar that supports common areas, shared loads, or tenant energy credits.

Solar energy options for renters

Renters have historically been locked out of solar. Without a roof to install on or a landlord willing to invest, going solar felt out of reach. New laws, new technology, and new programs are changing that.

Balcony solar panels

Balcony and portable solar can help renters access clean power where local rules, building safety, lease terms, and equipment standards allow. Senate Bill 868 (SB 868) is a policy signal to watch, not settled law.

500W–2kW output $500–2,500 cost Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) rebate eligible Senate Bill 868 (SB 868)

Window solar films

Developed by companies like NEXT Energy Technologies, transparent photovoltaic films can be applied directly to existing window glass, generating electricity while still letting in natural light. No drilling, no structural changes, no rooftop required.

Low profile No structural change Efficiency incentive eligible

Community solar subscription

Subscribe to a share of an off-site solar farm and receive a credit on your monthly electric bill based on the energy it produces. No installation, no equipment, and no roof access needed.

No installation needed Monthly subscription CA Community Solar program

Home battery storage

Portable battery units store solar energy, whether your own or from a community subscription, for use during evenings, peak pricing hours, or grid outages, giving renters reliable backup power.

$300–3,000 Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) rebates available Grid-independent backup

Policy signals landlords should track

California already requires qualifying large multifamily buildings to benchmark and report energy use, and the 2025 Energy Code continues the move toward efficient, electrified, solar-ready buildings. Senate Bill 868 (SB 868) is best treated as a policy watch item for renter-accessible plug-in solar rather than a settled owner mandate.

Landlords who plan early can benchmark performance, schedule replacements for aging gas equipment, compare rebates and financing, and avoid making rushed emergency upgrades that lock in higher operating costs.

Early adoption can also pay off commercially: efficient, resilient, solar-ready buildings are easier to market to renters who care about utility bills, heat waves, outages, indoor air, and climate impact.

Buildings that measure, plan, and share benefits with tenants will be better positioned than buildings that wait for the market and policy environment to force the conversation.
